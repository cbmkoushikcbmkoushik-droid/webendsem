About the Indian Constitution

The Constitution of India is the supreme law of India. It came into effect on January 26, 1950, making India a sovereign democratic republic.

ğŸ“œ What is the Constitution?

The Constitution is a set of fundamental principles and rules that define the powers and duties of the government and guarantee certain rights to its citizens.

âœï¸ Who Drafted it?

The Constitution was drafted by the Constituent Assembly headed by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, the Chairman of the Drafting Committee. It took 2 years, 11 months, and 18 days to draft.

âš–ï¸ Key Features

  • Federalism: Division of powers between Union and States
  • Parliamentary System: Government responsible to Parliament
  • Separation of Powers: Legislature, Executive, Judiciary
  • Rule of Law: No one is above the law
  • Fundamental Rights: Protection of citizens' rights

Structure of Government

The Indian Government is structured on the principle of separation of powers with three main branches.

âš–ï¸ Executive Branch

  • President: Constitutional head of state (5-year term)
  • Vice-President: Ex-officio Chairman of Rajya Sabha
  • Prime Minister: Head of Government, leader of majority
  • Council of Ministers: Cabinet ministers and ministers of state
  • Functions: Implementation of laws and policies

ğŸ“œ Legislative Branch

  • Lok Sabha: Lower house (House of People) - 543 elected members + 2 nominated
  • Rajya Sabha: Upper house (Council of States) - 245 members
  • Functions: Law-making, budget approval, public grievances
  • Term: Lok Sabha - 5 years, Rajya Sabha - 6 years (1/3 retire every 2 years)

âš”ï¸ Judicial Branch

  • Supreme Court: Apex court, Chief Justice + 30 judges
  • High Courts: One in each state, appellate and original jurisdiction
  • District Courts: Lower courts at district level
  • Functions: Interpretation of laws, judicial review, resolving disputes

ğŸ”„ Checks and Balances

  • No branch can exercise unlimited power
  • Each branch checks and balances the others
  • Ensures accountability and transparency
  • Protects democratic principles
